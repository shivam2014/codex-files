How airlines partner: from handshake to merger

Airlines don't just fly their own planes — they strike deals with competitors to reach more cities, fill more seats, and cut costs. The deals range from handshake-level ticket acceptance to full legal fusion. This page builds the foundation first, then walks each type.

What is an airline ticket?

A ticket is a contract between you and the airline. Today it's electronic (e-ticket) — you get a receipt with a ticket number and your flight numbers.

The ticket number is 13 digits. The first 3 identify which airline issued the ticket (e.g. 220 = LOT, 057 = American Airlines). The rest is a unique ID.

A flight number looks like LO 333. The 2-letter code (LO) is the airline's IATA code. The number identifies a specific route and time. The same flight can have multiple flight numbers if it's a codeshare — the plane only flies once, but each partner calls it something different.

Example: ticket 220-1234567890 for flights LO 333 WAW→FRA and SQ 325 FRA→SIN. The 220 tells you LOT issued this ticket.

Connecting flight vs Interline — they are NOT the same thing

Connecting flight describes a routing: you fly A→B, then B→C, changing planes. It says nothing about who operates the flights. A connection can be:

  • Online connection — same airline both legs. LOT WAW→FRA + LOT FRA→SIN. One ticket, one airline responsible end-to-end. No interline agreement needed.
  • Interline connection — two different airlines. LOT WAW→FRA + Singapore Airlines FRA→SIN. Requires an interline agreement between LOT and SQ.
  • Self-connection — you buy two separate tickets. LOT WAW→FRA on one booking, SQ FRA→SIN on another. This is NOT interline. If LOT is delayed, SQ has no obligation to help you. You must collect bags and re-check at FRA.

Interline is specifically the commercial agreement that lets two airlines sell connecting itineraries on a single ticket. It's what turns a self-connection into a protected connection.

Same routing, different treatment:
You fly JFK→LHR (BA) + LHR→PRG (Czech Airlines). If you buy one ticket → interline connection. BA and OK have an interline agreement. Your bag is checked through to PRG. If BA is delayed, OK rebooks you.
If you buy two separate tickets → self-connection. You must collect bags at LHR, re-check with OK. If BA is delayed, you lose your OK ticket. Same airports, same flights — different agreement.

What is IATA?

IATA (International Air Transport Association) is the airline industry's rulebook. It's a trade body that almost all airlines belong to.

IATA defines the standards that make partnerships possible:

  • Ticket format — so every airline can read another's ticket
  • Baggage rules — so bags transfer seamlessly
  • BSP — a clearing house that settles money between airlines
  • Airline codes — every airline gets a unique 2-letter code (LO = LOT, AA = American, SQ = Singapore)
Think of IATA as the person who writes the rules of chess. Airlines follow those rules, so when LOT issues a ticket for a Singapore Airlines flight, both know exactly how to handle it.

Marketing carrier vs Operating carrier

When two airlines partner, one sells the seat and the other flies it.

  • Marketing carrier — puts their flight number on it, sells it on their website, owns the customer relationship. They might not fly the plane at all.
  • Operating carrier — owns the plane, provides the crew, does the maintenance, handles the meals. Their name is on the safety card.

In an interline, each airline is operator for its own sectors. In a codeshare, the marketing carrier sells the operator's seat under its own flight number. In a JV, they both market and operate but share everything.

You book LO 5801 on LOT's website. The plane is ANA's, with ANA crew and ANA meals. LOT is the marketing carrier. ANA is the operating carrier.

GDS — the booking switchboard

GDS (Global Distribution System) is the computer network that connects travel agents, airline websites, and hotel systems. The big three are Amadeus, Sabre, and Travelport (Galileo).

When you search for a flight on LOT's website, LOT's system sends a query to Amadeus (or whichever GDS it uses). Amadeus asks all connected airlines and returns available flights, prices, and seats — even for airlines that aren't LOT.

This is what makes interline and codeshare possible: the GDS knows that "LO 5801" is really "NH 1234 operated by ANA" and handles the connection seamlessly.

Think of GDS as a telephone switchboard operator. When you say "connect me to a flight to Tokyo," the GDS rings every airline at once and tells you who answers.

BSP & Prorate — how airlines split the money

When you buy a ticket that uses two different airlines, who gets paid how much?

BSP (Billing and Settlement Plan, run by IATA) is the clearing house. Travel agents pay BSP once a month for all tickets sold. BSP then distributes the money to each airline. Simple.

Prorate is the formula for splitting one ticket's revenue between multiple airlines. The standard method: each airline gets a share based on the distance they fly relative to the total. If WAW→FRA is 500 miles and FRA→SIN is 6,000 miles, the first airline gets ~8%, the second gets ~92%.

An SPA (Special Prorate Agreement) is a custom deal — two airlines agree to split revenue differently on a specific route, often used in codeshares.

You pay $1,000 for a WAW→FRA (LOT) + FRA→SIN (SQ) ticket. BSP collects $1,000. Prorate says LOT gets ~$80 (shorter flight), SQ gets ~$920 (longer flight).

AOC — the license to fly

AOC (Air Operator Certificate) is the government-issued document that says "this company is legally allowed to operate aircraft." Every airline has its own AOC.

To get one, an airline must prove to its civil aviation authority (e.g., FAA in the US, EASA in Europe) that it has qualified pilots, a maintenance program, proper procedures, insurance — the whole safety infrastructure.

This is important in partnerships because even when airlines codeshare or JV, each airline still operates under its own AOC. The marketing carrier cannot fly the operating carrier's planes — only the operating carrier's AOC allows that. In a merger, the two AOCs eventually merge into one.

After LATAM's merger, LAN (Chile) and TAM (Brazil) kept separate AOCs for 3 years. They couldn't fly each other's planes until both aviation authorities approved a single LATAM AOC.

Revenue pool & metal-neutral

In a Joint Venture, airlines stop competing on certain routes and instead agree to pool all revenue from those routes into one shared pot.

Metal-neutral means it doesn't matter which airline's aircraft ("metal") carried the passenger. If BA flies 80% of JV passengers and AA flies 20%, but the pool is split 50:50 — AA gets more than its "share" of flights, and BA gets less. That's the point: both airlines stop fighting over who flies and instead optimise the total network together.

The pool typically deducts operating costs (fuel, crew, airport fees) and splits the profit quarterly. An independent auditor ensures fairness.

British Airways and American Airlines pool all JFK→LHR revenue. BA flies 7 daily flights, AA flies 3. But they agreed a 50:50 split. If AA fills its 3 flights at higher fares, its passengers' revenue flows to both.

Antitrust immunity (ATI)

Normally, competing airlines cannot legally agree on prices or capacity. That would be a cartel — price-fixing — and it's illegal in the US, EU, and most countries.

Antitrust immunity (ATI) is permission from regulators (US Department of Transportation, European Commission) to coordinate pricing, schedules, and capacity on specific routes without being sued for collusion.

Regulators grant ATI only when the airlines prove the partnership will benefit consumers: more frequencies, better connections, lower fares. ATI is reviewed periodically and can be revoked.

The Atlantic JV (BA, AA, Iberia, Finnair) has ATI from both US and EU. Without it, BA and AA couldn't sit in a room and agree "you fly at 17:00, we'll fly at 19:00." With ATI, they can.

Interline Agreement

Loose · bilateral

Two airlines agree to accept each other's tickets on connecting flights. You book one itinerary, fly LOT from Warsaw to Frankfurt, then Singapore Airlines from Frankfurt to Bangkok — all on a single ticket. Baggage is checked through. If your first flight is delayed, the interline agreement means the second airline is notified and rebooks you.

⚠️ Common confusion: "connecting flight" and "interline" are not the same thing. A connecting flight is a routing — you fly A→B→C. The connection can be on the same airline (online), on two airlines with one ticket (interline), or on two separate tickets (self-connect). Interline is specifically the agreement that lets two airlines sell one ticket together. Think of it this way: the interline connection panel in the foundations above shows all three side by side.

Trace a passenger through an interline

Passenger P books a single ticket for LOT WAW→FRA connecting to Singapore Airlines FRA→SIN.

  1. Booking: LOT issues one e-ticket number (e.g. 220-1234567890). The ticket number starts with 220 — that's LOT's IATA code, meaning LOT is the airline that sold you the ticket. The ticket contains both flights: LO 333 and SQ 325.
  2. The GDS connects them: LOT's reservation system uses a GDS (Amadeus) to check seat availability on SQ's flight. The GDS links LOT's and SQ's systems so LOT can sell SQ seats.
  3. Check-in at WAW: LOT agent sees the full WAW→FRA→SIN itinerary. They check bags through to SIN, attach a tag that says "SIN via FRA". The bag tag follows IATA standards so SQ's ground handlers at FRA can read it.
  4. At FRA connection: Passenger walks off LOT's plane at FRA and walks to SQ's gate. The bag is transferred by airport ground handlers. No re-check-in, no new ticket. The interline agreement makes both airlines responsible.
  5. Settlement: LOT and SQ don't just trust each other. IATA's BSP collects the ticket money. LOT and SQ split it using a prorate formula — roughly by each sector's distance.
🔗 Real example: British Airways and JetBlue have an interline agreement. You buy one ticket for BA LHR→JFK connecting to JetBlue JFK→BOS. JetBlue isn't in the Oneworld alliance but still accepts BA tickets through their bilateral interline deal.

Codeshare Agreement

Moderate · marketing

One airline sells seats on another airline's flights under its own flight number. The plane, crew, and meals belong to the operating carrier. The flight number on your boarding pass belongs to the marketing carrier. Codeshare is deeper than interline because schedules are intentionally coordinated and a custom SPA (Special Prorate Agreement) replaces the automatic prorate formula.

Trace a passenger through a codeshare

You book LOT LO 5801 Warsaw→Tokyo Narita on lot.com. The plane is an A350 operated by ANA (All Nippon Airways).

  1. Booking on lot.com: You search LOT's website for WAW→NRT. The results show LO5801. Below the flight number, small text reads: "operated by ANA". You pay LOT in PLN. ANA's actual flight number for the same plane is NH 1234 — two flight numbers, one plane.
  2. Behind the scenes: LOT's website queries the GDS (Amadeus) for ANA's seat inventory. Amadeus knows LO5801 = NH1234. It shows ANA's available seats under LOT's flight number. When you buy, Amadeus reserves seat 14A on ANA's flight.
  3. Check-in: You check in at LOT's counter at WAW. LOT prints a boarding pass showing both flight numbers: LO 5801 / NH 1234. The seat map on the screen is ANA's — LOT doesn't control seat assignments on ANA's plane.
  4. At the gate and onboard: The gate staff are ANA's (or a ground-handling agent working for ANA). Onboard, crew wear ANA uniforms, safety card says ANA, meal is Japanese. You are an ANA passenger — the only difference is the flight number on your boarding pass.
  5. Settlement with SPA: LOT and ANA have an SPA. LOT keeps ~15% of what you paid as a "marketing fee." ANA gets ~85% for operating. Compare to interline prorate: in codeshare, the marketing carrier gets a fixed fee rather than a per-distance cut.
🔗 Real example: Delta DL 7000-series flights across the Atlantic are codeshares on Virgin Atlantic metal. Emirates EK 241 Dubai→Sydney is a codeshare on Qantas QF 8417. You buy from Emirates but fly Qantas.

Joint Venture

Deep · metal-neutral

The deepest cooperation without merging. On a set of routes, airlines pool revenue, coordinate schedules, and jointly decide capacity. They operate under antitrust immunity (ATI) — without it, joint pricing would be illegal. The key concept: metal-neutral — it doesn't matter whose plane flies your route; all revenue goes into one pool and is split by formula.

Trace a passenger through a Joint Venture

The Atlantic JV (American Airlines, British Airways, Iberia, Finnair). You book BA from JFK→LHR→PRG on ba.com.

  1. Schedule coordination: BA and AA don't compete on JFK→LHR. They meet quarterly (with ATI protection) and decide: "BA runs 2 daily flights, AA runs 3 daily — total 5 frequencies, staggered every 2 hours." Without the JV, each would run 3 flights, trying to steal share, costing both airlines money on empty seats.
  2. Revenue pooling: Your $1,200 fare goes into the Atlantic JV pool — not to BA's bank account. At the end of each quarter, an independent auditor adds up all JV ticket revenue, subtracts operating costs (fuel, crew, airport fees), and splits the profit 50:50 between BA and AA.
  3. Metal-neutral in action: Say BA flies 80% of JV passengers but the pool split is 50:50. AA gets paid for passengers it didn't fly, and BA gets less than its "share." Both accept this because they jointly optimise — BA's strong London hub plus AA's strong US network together attract more total passengers.
  4. Joint pricing: BA and AA set fares together. A JFK→LHR fare on ba.com and aa.com will be the same. They don't undercut each other. This would be illegal price-fixing without ATI.
🔗 Real examples: Star Alliance Atlantic JV (United, Lufthansa, Air Canada). Delta–Virgin Atlantic–KLM–Air France JV — the most complex multi-carrier JV. China Southern and American Airlines have a transpacific JV.

Merger / Acquisition

Full · legal consolidation

Two airlines become one legal entity. One brand survives (or a new one is created), one AOC (Air Operator Certificate), one CEO, one P&L. This is the end of the partnership spectrum. But it's also the hardest: merging IT systems cost $150M+ in some cases, pilot seniority list integration can take years, and cultural clashes can destroy synergies.

Trace what happens in a merger

LATAM Airlines (LAN Chile + TAM Brazil, 2012). Not a partnership — a full legal fusion. Seven real things that happened:

  1. Legal structure: LAN (Chile) acquired TAM (Brazil). A holding company, LATAM Airlines Group SA, was created. For 3 years, both airlines kept their own AOCs — LAN couldn't fly TAM's planes and vice versa. Only in 2015 did regulators approve a single LATAM AOC.
  2. Fleet standardisation: Before: LAN flew Airbus A320 + Boeing 787. TAM flew Airbus A320 + A330 + A350 + Boeing 767. After merger, LATAM retired all Boeing widebodies early. Now: all-Airbus fleet (A320 family for short haul, A330/A350 for long haul). Common fleet → common parts → cheaper maintenance.
  3. IT systems: LAN used Sabre reservation system. TAM used Amadeus. Merging took 4 years and cost ~$150M. During transition, a passenger flying GRU→SCL could hit two different check-in systems depending on which legacy side the flight belonged to. Today: one unified Sabre instance.
  4. Labour integration: Two pilot seniority lists with different pay scales, retirement ages, and union rules (Brazilian vs Chilean labour law). Arbitrators spent 2 years negotiating a merged list. The final formula credited years of service proportionally — a 10-year TAM pilot didn't lose seniority to a 5-year LAN pilot.
  5. Brand phase-in: Both LAN and TAM liveries flew for 5 years. Repainting a widebody costs ~$200K. LATAM's strategy: repaint during heavy maintenance checks (every 6-8 years) to avoid taking a plane out of service just for paint.
🔗 Real examples: American Airlines + US Airways (2013) — created the world's largest airline. Air France–KLM (2004) — "merger of equals" kept both hub brands. Delta + Northwest (2008) — considered the gold standard: Delta's operating playbook replaced Northwest's, teething problems for 6 months, then strong synergy delivery.

FAQ

Can a codeshare exist without an interline?
Technically no — a codeshare includes interline functionality (ticket acceptance, baggage transfer). But the reverse is common: interline without codeshare. Many low-cost carriers (like JetBlue) have interline agreements for connections but don't put their code on partner flights.
What's the difference between an alliance and a Joint Venture?
Alliances (Star Alliance, Oneworld, SkyTeam) are marketing clubs. They set standards for:
  • Status recognition (Gold members access lounges across all member airlines)
  • Frequent flyer miles earning and burning across members
  • Basic interline connections between members
But alliances do not involve joint pricing, revenue pooling, or coordinated capacity. A JV is much deeper. It requires antitrust immunity and involves joint pricing, capacity coordination, and revenue pooling — things an alliance alone cannot do.

Example: Singapore Airlines is in Star Alliance but has no transatlantic JV. So on routes to the US, it coordinates schedules with United at a basic level but doesn't pool revenue or jointly price with them.
Do JV airlines still compete with each other?
On JV-covered routes: they don't compete — they coordinate. On non-JV routes: they compete freely. For example, BA and AA coordinate on JFK→LHR (JV route) but BA might compete with AA on LHR→Dubai (not typically in the Atlantic JV scope). Regulators approve JVs specifically to prevent collusion from spreading beyond the agreed routes.
Why merge if you can do a JV?
JVs keep each airline's union contracts, pension liabilities, and balance sheets separate. Mergers unlock deeper savings:
  • One IT system instead of maintaining two connected
  • Combined purchasing power (aircraft orders, fuel hedging, catering)
  • Single maintenance organisation
  • Shared back-office (HR, finance, legal)
But mergers carry serious risk: failed IT integration, culture clashes, regulatory forced sell-offs. JVs are cheaper and reversible. Mergers are permanent.
Do budget / low-cost airlines use these partnerships?
Traditionally no — low-cost carriers (LCCs) deliberately avoided interline/codeshare because it adds complexity. If your flight is delayed and you miss a partner connection, who pays for the hotel? LCCs didn't want that liability.

But this is changing:
  • JetBlue has interline and codeshare deals with BA, Turkish, Aer Lingus
  • Southwest does interline baggage transfer (not ticketing) with some partners
  • As LCCs grow and add more connecting traffic, they're moving up the partnership spectrum
How do I know if my flight is a codeshare?
When booking, look for the fine print. Any reputable booking site or airline website will show:
  • "Operated by [Airline Name]" below the flight number
  • A different airline name at the gate or on the aircraft
  • Two flight numbers on your boarding pass (e.g. LO 5801 / NH 1234)
The giveaway: if you're flying LOT but the aircraft in the seat selection screen shows a different airline's layout, it's a codeshare.
